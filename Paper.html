General idea

The Sid Meier’s Civilization series is a wildly popular videogame presenting world history to a varied audience. The idea of using a videogame to communicate truths has long seemed like an impossibility, but some scholars have recently started to embrace it. The greatest strength of historical videogames is to represent history as a process. By giving a player agency in historical events, it teaches the player about cause and effect.1 Most people who have played the Civilization series might agree that this is, to some extent, the truth. However, the immensly popular series has received its fair share of criticism, both from historical scholars and professional game critics. Alister MacQuarrie recently criticized the fact that Civilization is ‘all rise and no fall’2, presenting an overly optimistic view of history. Kanishk Taroor argued that Sid Meier’s ideal of making the series apolitical was impossible.3 Others have pointed out the overrepresentation of certain continents in the series’ available civilizations and wonders.4 After the recent announcement of the Rise and Fall expansion for Civilization VI, which included the nation of the Cree, the headman of the Cree reached out to the game developers to criticize the way his people were portrayed. He complained that the game reïnforced the idea that the Cree people had the same mindset as the European Settlers.5 This echoes a commonly heard sentiment about the game: not matter who you start out as, your civilization will eventually end up looking like modern day America. Some of these criticisms could only be addressed by radically reshaping some of the game-mechanics of the series. But what improvements could be made within the existing framework of the games? The series has had a very active modding community since the release of the fourth installment of the series. Could these modders, who theoretically do not face the problem of having to comply with studio bosses, play a part in alleviating some of these criticisms? This website offers a list of tested modifications available on the steam workshop. Each mod is evaluated on four aspects:

  • Accuracy
    How historically acurate is the mod? Are there any truly glaring errors?
  • Diversity
    Does the mod expand the options offered by Civilization?
  • Transparency
    Does the mod credit a source for used information? Do elements added by it have a Civipedia entry?
  • Compatability
    Does the mod force the user to install other mods? Does it change the game in a radical way that decreases compatability?

It is important to be realistic about what modding can and cannot do for us. Technically, modding could allow people to edit Civilization VI in order to make it more 'historical'. However, both modding and historical research are time-consuming activities. It would take a very dedicated community to do this. Furthermore, the 'free market' of the Steam Workshop does not necessarily provide us with a broader perspective than the Game Developers do. To illustrate this, one could look at the origins of the leaders in the base-game of Civilization VI. It has often been remarked that the choices of leaders are very euro- or eurasian-centric. Unfortunately, this tendency is copied by the modders on the Steam Workshop, as illustrated by this graph. Included are only the historical leaders; modifications that add, for example, Lord Vetinari or similair fictional characters to the game are excluded. The other category includes Australia and hard-to classify locations such as the arctic regions. This graph shows that, although the steam workshop does offer a little more variety than the game developers did, the focus is still heavily slanted towards European and Asian leaders.
